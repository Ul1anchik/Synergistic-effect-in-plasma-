# Synergistic-effect-in-plasma


In the context of thermonuclear fusion research in tokamaks, the induction-free method of generating plasma current is of particular interest. An effective way of achieving this is to use low-hybrid waves.

The primary mechanism by which energy is transferred from an electromagnetic wave to electrons in plasma is Landau damping. This process involves the transfer of longitudinal momentum from lower-hybrid waves to plasma electrons. The wave transfers energy to the electrons most efficiently when their thermal velocity equals the wave's phase velocity. When waves interact with electrons, the distribution function undergoes quasi-linear deformation, forming a 'plateau' in the velocity range corresponding to the wave's phase velocity. This generates a directed current without the need for an induced electric field.

During the discharge, RF waves with a total power output of 4 MW were injected into the plasma. The wave deceleration spectra were obtained using the **Grill3D programme**.

When considering individual parts of the spectrum, it is important to trace the contribution to current generation and how it differs from the results obtained when considering all peaks together.
The left wing of the spectrum consists of four distinct peaks whose decelerations correspond to a positive contribution to the generated RF current. The right wing corresponds to a negative contribution to the drive current and does not affect the results for the left wing, since the model assumes independent calculations for negative and positive directions. Calculations were performed for all peaks, both individually (taking the right wing into account) and collectively.

As can be seen clearly from the tables below, the current contribution from individual peaks is less than the combined current contribution from the entire spectrum.

A particularly clear example of the synergistic effect is the consideration of the combined influence on LH current from two peaks. For example, for antenna phasing Δφ = 0: the first peak of the left wing gives 726 kA, the second peak gives 23 kA, but together they give 757 kA. Similarly for the peaks on the right side: the first gives -298 kA, the second gives -16 kA, but together they give -473 kA.
